* (54) Title: APPARATUS FOR PLAYING A BOARD GAME
* (57) Abstract  
  Apparatus for playing a board game comprises a board (30) having a number of home-base locations (1, 2), a number of
  interconnecting pathways (4) extending between the home-base locations (1, 2) which define stopping points (3) at the
  interconnections of the pathways. The apparatus also comprises a number first playing pieces (9) for each player such
  that each player's first playing pieces (9) are adapted to be distinguished from other player's first playing pieces
  (9) and each player's first playing pieces are initially positioned at a home-base location, 2) and are advanced along
  the pathways via the stopping points (3) towards another predetermined home-base location (2). Whenever one player's
  playing pieces (5) are moved into a predetermined relationship with one or more of another player's playing pieces (
  6), that one or another player's playing pieces are removed from that one or another player's control, as the case may
  be.
  Typically, the apparatus further comprises a number of second playing pieces (10) for each player such that each
  player's second playing pieces are adapted to distinguish from other player's second playing pieces and from that
  player's first playing pieces. The second playing pieces (10) replace each of the first playing pieces (9) that
  reaches a predetermined home-base location and may be advanced along the pathways (4) via the stopping points (3) in
  any direction.

## APPARATUS FOR PLAYING A BOARD GAME

This invention relates to apparatus for playing a board game.

According to the present invention there is provided apparatus for playing a board game comprising a board having a
number of home-base locations, a number of interconnecting pathways extending between the home-base locations and
stopping points defined by the interconnections of the pathways; the apparatus further comprising a number of first
playing pieces for each player, each player's first playing pieces being capable of being distinguished from other
players' first playing pieces, and for initial positioning at the home-base locations and for advancing along the
pathways via the stopping points.

The invention enables the board game be played so that whenever one player's playing pieces are moved into a
predetermined relationship with one or more of another player's playing pieces, that one or another player's playing
pieces are removed from that one or another player's control, as the case may be.

In the preferred embodiment the board has two home-base locations, although in an alternative embodiment the board may
have six home-base locations. Preferably, the stopping points are circular, although they could be a diamond shape or
any other suitable shape, or even marked only by the interconnection of the pathways. Preferably, the pathways are
rectilinear. The pathways typically comprise lines which are marked on the board. Alternatively the lines may be used to
define regions which are used as stopping points, the pieces being moved across the lines into adjacent regions.

Typically, each first playing piece has a number of player indicating sides which carry respective different player
indicators, whereby each piece can be placed on the board with any of its player indicating sides uppermost.

In the preferred embodiment there are two player indicating sides on each first playing piece, each player indicating
side being a different colour. Preferably one side is black and the other is white, although the sides may be strongly
contrasting shades of another colour.

In an alternative embodiment each first playing piece is a cube which has six player indicating sides which are
different colours.

Preferably, there is at least as many player indicating sides on each first playing piece as there are home-base
locations on the board.

Preferably, the playing pieces for each player are for advancing along the pathways via the stopping points only in a
direction towards the other of the home-base locations, respectively. The apparatus also preferably includes a number of
second playing pieces for each player, with each player's second pieces being capable of being distinguished from other
player's second playing pieces and from the first playing pieces, for replacing each of the first playing pieces that
reaches the other of the home-base locations and for advancing along the pathways via the stopping points in any
direction.

Typically, each second playing piece two player indicating sides, each player indicating side having a major player
indicating section and a minor section for distinguishing the piece from a first playing piece. Preferably the major and
minor sections are different colours, although the minor section could be provided by an emblem or other distinguishing
mark.

Alternatively, each of the first playing pieces may be advanced along the pathways via the stopping points in any
direction after having reached another predetermined home-base location and wherein the first playing pieces are adapted
to indicate whether they are intended to be advanced only in a direction towards a predetermined home-base location or
in any direction.

Referring to Figure 1, the playing board comprises two home-bases `1`, `2` located at opposite ends of the board and
each consisting of four stopping points, such as shown at `3`, joined by four pathways in the form of lines, such as
shown at `4`, in the formation of a square. An imaginary extension both sides of the line between the outermost stopping
points at each end is called the "home-base line". Between the two home-bases is a network of 34 interconnecting
pathways also in the form of lines defining the locations of 15 further stopping points at the interconnections of the
lines. The lines are all of equal length, so that the stopping points are equally spaced from all adjacent stopping
points, the term "adjacent" stopping points being used to define stopping points linked by a single line and
"non-adjacent" stopping points therefore meaning stopping points linked by two or more intervening lines and at least
one other stopping point.

The lines in the central region of the network form a regular hexagon with opposite corners joined by straight lines to
form equilateral triangles. An outer square is formed on each side of the hexagon and the other adjacent corners of the
squares are joined by further lines, including one from each of the home-bases, to form six more equilateral triangles
to complete a regular dodecagon.

For small or pocket-size boards, the length of each line is preferably 2.5 cm and the overall length of the
representation on the board is preferably 14.33 cm. In medium-sized boards, the length of the representation is
preferably about 32 cm and in large size boards preferably about 40 cm.

One set of playing pieces consists of a plurality of disc-shaped, reversible tokens, known as "common" pieces or "cobs",
uniformly constructed so as to be indistinguishable from each other. Each face `5`, `6` of one of these tokens `9`,
shown in Figure 2, wherein it can be seen that one face `5` is coloured a dark colour and the other face `6` is coloured
a light colour. Pieces with the light-coloured side facing upwards are known as "white" and pieces with the
dark-coloured side facing upwards are known as "black". The light and dark colours may actually be white and black,
respectively, or any two strongly contrasting shades of the same colour.

A second set of playing pieces consists of a plurality of tokens `10`, each face `11`, `12` of one of which is shown in
Figure 3. It can be seen that these pieces are constructed and coloured similar to the "cob" pieces `9`, except for a
contrasting mark, `7`, `8`, such as a centrally-positioned spot, or an emblem on each face `11`, `12` thereof. The
playing pieces of the second set are known as "royal" pieces or "roks".

The diameters of all the playing pieces should be the same and related to the board size. Preferably, a line between
adjacent stopping points on the board is of length between two-and one-eighth and two-and one-quarter of the diameter of
the playing pieces, so that one playing piece should be able to pass closely, but comfortably, between two other pieces
placed centrally on adjacent points without touching either of them. The playing pieces of both sets are preferably of
thickness not more than half of their diameter for use on small boards and not less than one-fifth of their diameter for
use on medium or large size boards. The width of the lines should be not more than one-eighth of the diameter of the
pieces, and the stopping points, if emphasised, should be of diameter less than one half of the diameter of the playing
pieces. The overall length of the representation on the board is determined as S(4 + tan 60°), where S is the distance
between adjacent points. The board may be painted, printed, or inlaid in solid material, and the representation and
background are of contrasting shades, which are preferably the same as, or similar to, those used for the playing
pieces. Additional marks or colours may also be used to distinguish each end of the board.

The board and playing pieces are designed and constructed for two players, each being associated with one of the two
colours of the playing pieces and one of the home-bases. The board and playing pieces, in accordance with the invention,
may be used to play several different games, one aim of which typically is to capture or immobilise the opponent's
playing pieces. The rules for each of the games are as follows.

In the first game, eight reversible "cob" pieces `9` and eight reversible "rok" pieces `10` are used.

At the beginning of the first game, a white "cob" piece is placed on each of the four stopping points of one home-base
and a black "cob" piece on each of the four points of the other home-base. The players move their pieces alternately,
with the white "cob" pieces playing first, by advancing a "cob" piece from one stopping point along a line in the
direction of the opponent's home-base to an adjacent stopping point. A "cob" piece can only be moved to a point nearer
the opponent's "home-base line" except when such a piece begins a move on one of its own home-base stopping points, in
which case it can be moved in any direction to effect a capture, as described hereinafter. No piece may be moved to a
stopping point occupied by another piece.

When a piece is moved from a non-adjacent stopping point to a stopping point adjacent an opponent's piece, it is said to
have captured that piece. The captured piece remains on the same stopping point but is turned over, so that its opposite
face is upwards, to show the opposite colour, and subsequently belongs to the player who has captured it.

More than one piece may be captured by the same move, but no piece may be captured by a piece which was adjacent it
before the move.

A "cob" piece is promoted to a "rok" piece when it is advanced onto an opponent's home-base stopping point, and the "
cob" piece is removed from the board and a "rok" piece having the same colour facing upwards is substituted. A "cob"
piece captured on one of its own home-base stopping points is not immediately promoted, but becomes a "cob" piece of the
opposite colour by turning it over on the opponent's home-base point. It is then promoted if it is later advanced.

Unlike a "cob" piece, a "rok" piece can move in any direction along the lines.

A "cob" piece on one of its opponent's two outermost home-base stopping points cannot be advanced and is said to be "
dead". A "cob" piece, the only legal path of which is blocked by a "dead" piece, is also "dead". A piece is not
necessarily "dead" if it cannot be moved. For example, no "rok" piece can be "dead", and a "cob" piece, the path of
which is blocked by a "cob" piece of the opposite colour, or by a "live" "cob" piece of the same colour, or by a "rok"
piece of either colour, is not "dead". A player, who cannot move any of his pieces, but who possesses one or more "dead"
pieces, may promote one of them to a "rok" piece, if this permits it to be moved. A player with a "cob" piece, which is
the sole remaining piece of its colour, must promote it in any case, wherever it is positioned.

The game terminates in a win for one player or a draw. A player wins if, firstly, the opponent cannot legally move any
of his pieces, or secondly the opponent resigns or abandons the game, or thirdly, if a time limit is imposed for
performing a set number of moves, the opponent fails to complete the required number of moves in the time limit. The
game is drawn if firstly the players agree a draw. Secondly, the game is drawn if the moves of the game have been
recorded and a player can prove that at least fifty consecutive moves, including the move last played, have been played
by each player without moving or promoting a "cob" piece. This draw may not however be claimed, regardless of the count,
if a player has already won the game or can win, because the opponent cannot legally move any piece, at the next
opportunity to play. Thirdly, the game is drawn if a player correctly announces that, at the completion of his next
intended move, the resulting position will have occurred at least three times. Pieces of the same rank and colour must
occupy identical stopping points and it must be the same player's turn to play on each occasion. If the claim is
incorrect, the announced move, if legal, must be played and the game continues without prejudice to the claimant's right
to re-claim the draw at a later stage. The moves can be recorded using any suitable notations and by labelling or
numbering the stopping points, for example, in a similar manner to map references.

In the second game, only one set of playing pieces is used, preferably consisting of 22 reversible pieces having light
and dark colours on respective faces.

As with the first game, each player chooses a light or dark colour and places a piece on each of his four home-base
stopping points. Again, the player with the lighter coloured pieces begins by advancing a piece along a line to an
adjacent stopping point. Also, in accordance with the "cob" pieces of the first game, the pieces must move towards the
opponent's "home-base line" and no piece can move to a stopping point already occupied.

If a piece finishes its move adjacent one or more opposing pieces, the player must pay that number of pieces, known as "
penalty" pieces, to the opponent, by placing them on stopping points on the board, except that they may not be placed
adjacent any of the player's own pieces or nearer the player's home base-line than the opponent's most advanced piece.
If the player cannot place all the "penalty" pieces, he must place as many as possible and then pay the difference by
removing his own pieces from the board. If it not possible for a player to advance any of his pieces, he must remove one
of them from the board. A player wins when the opponent has no pieces left on the board.

If a position is repeated, with neither player willing to break the sequence of moves that led to it, some means
involving chance, such as a coin or dice, may be used to determine which player must break the sequence. The winner of
matches, where more than one game is played, can be decided by counting either the number of games won, or the number of
pieces remaining on the board when each game has, which are then used as points towards a final score for the match.

Whilst a particular embodiment has been described, it will be understood that modifications can be made without
departure from the scope of the invention. For example, some of the lines of the board can be curved rather than
straight, so as to form circles, instead of the hexagon and dodecagon, or alternatively any other formation of
interconnecting lines and points may be utilised. Also, the "stopping points" which are emphasised on the representation
shown in the figure, may be a shape other than circular, such as a square or diamond. Alternatively, the stopping points
may be formed merely by the meeting of the lines, with no specific emphasis thereof.

Instead of using reversible playing pieces, playing pieces having a uniform colour may be used, with different coloured
pieces being provided for each player, in which case twice the number of pieces for two players will be required. In
this way, more than two players could play at any one time and also more than two home-bases can be provided around the
periphery of the board. Alternatively, for the first game, each playing piece could be uniformly coloured with an emblem
or other contrasting mark on one face thereof, so that a single playing piece can be used as both a "cob" piece "rok"
piece for each player.

Playing pieces having more than two sides may be used for more than two players, with each face being differently
coloured or otherwise marked for each player. In this way, for example, a cubic playing piece, `13` as shown in Figure
4, with each of its faces (only three shown) being differently coloured may be used for up to six players, each player
having his colour facing upwards when the playing piece is on the board. A particular modification of the board for up
to six players is shown by the diagrammatic representation in Figure 5. It can be seen that the board `25` has six
home-base locations equally spaced around the board, with a larger network of lines `4` therebetween.

In a game using the board `25` and cubic playing pieces `13` shown in Figures 4 and 5, the players begin by each placing
four playing pieces with their respective coloured face facing upwards, on their respective home-base. If less than six
players are playing, home-bases spaced as evenly as possible around the board should be selected. The players advance
their playing pieces, in turn, along the lines `4` to the stopping points `3`, as in the above-described games, in any
direction, except that an initial move from a home-base stopping point must be along one of the lines forming a square
immediately adjacent that home-base. Playing pieces are captured, as in the above-described games, by a player's playing
piece being moved adjacent one or more of another player's playing pieces. The other players' cubic playing pieces are
then turned over so that the first player's colour is facing upward. A player who cannot move any of his playing pieces
or all his playing pieces have been captured retires from the game. If this player has any of his pieces remaining on
the board, they are removed. When only two players remain in the game, the game ends and the winner is the one with the
most pieces left on the board, or if the number of pieces are equal, both players share the winning position.
